Review

In Amaran, Gentleman Cadet Mukund Varadarajan marches during his passing-out parade as Indhu Rebecca Varghese excitedly cheers for him, her joy uncontainable. GV Prakash Kumar’s stirring score amplifies the moment, culminating in Mukund’s subtle half-smile—leaving us wondering if it’s for Indhu or the fulfillment of his dream to join the Indian Army. Director Rajkumar Periasamy suggests Mukund saw no difference between the two, adding depth to his story.

The film opens with a somber Indhu (a brilliant Sai Pallavi) heading somewhere significant. Set in Shopian, where Major Mukund serves in the 44 Rashtriya Rifles—a unit reflecting India’s diversity—the narrative shifts back to explore Indhu and Mukund’s love story. Sai Pallavi shines in the charming, playful moments, while Sivakarthikeyan feels more natural in uniform. Though Indhu’s Tamil-Malayalam dialogue takes time to settle, Pallavi balances cuteness and authenticity, anchoring the romance.
